## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This strategic document establishes Eurus Systems' vertical integration philosophy and make/buy framework for the MAAP-1 tandem-rotor heavy-lift autonomous helicopter program. The strategy balances the imperatives of a **Firm Fixed Price (FFP) contract** structure with the need for speed, technical control, IP protection, and sustainable competitive advantage.

**Strategic Recommendation:** Pursue **selective vertical integration** focused on **core differentiating technologies** (autonomy, rotor systems, flight controls) while strategically partnering on commoditized systems and long-lead specialty items (engines, avionics). This approach positions MAAP-1 for:

- **Margin protection** under FFP contracts through cost predictability on 40-45% of aircraft value
- **Schedule control** by internalizing critical path items prone to supplier delays
- **IP ownership** of autonomous flight technology as foundation for product family growth
- **Competitive moat** via proprietary integration know-how and rapid iteration capability

**Financial Impact:** Vertical integration strategy drives **$850M capital investment** (facilities, tooling, workforce) over 7 years, with **breakeven at aircraft #62** and **27% IRR** over 250-unit production life. The approach reduces **per-unit cost by 18%** vs. traditional prime contractor model by FRP Phase 2, directly supporting FFP contract profitability.

## 1. STRATEGIC CONTEXT AND PROGRAM IMPERATIVES

### 1.1 Program Business Model

The MAAP-1 program operates under unique constraints that fundamentally shape the vertical integration calculus:

| Constraint | Implication for Integration Strategy |
|------------|-------------------------------------|
| **Firm Fixed Price Contract** | Cost overruns directly erode margin; predictability > optimization |
| **Autonomous Mission-Critical Systems** | IP protection essential; external dependencies = competitive risk |
| **18 units/year at FRP (2030)** | Volume justifies dedicated production lines for high-value assemblies |
| **3 Variant Architecture** | Common "Green Aircraft" (80% commonality) enables integration economies of scale |
| **Export Market Potential** | Control of ITAR-sensitive systems (autonomy, rotor, flight controls) supports international sales without redesign |
| **New Entrant Market Position** | Vertical integration signals capability and reduces reliance on incumbent suppliers who may favor competitors |

### 1.2 Competitive Landscape Assessment

**Industry Vertical Integration Benchmarks:**

| Company | Integration Level | Core In-House Capabilities | Strategic Rationale |
|---------|------------------|---------------------------|---------------------|
| **SpaceX** | ~80% (Rockets/Dragon) | Engines, avionics, structures, software | Speed, cost, IP control; "build what you can't buy fast/cheap" |

**MAAP-1 Strategic Positioning:** Target **50-55% vertical integration** by value (between Anduril and traditional defense OEMs), reflecting:
- High software/autonomy content (similar to Anduril)
- Complex mechanical systems requiring OEM expertise (rotors, transmissions)
- Firm commitment to cost/schedule control under FFP contract

### 1.3 FFP Contract Margin Protection Framework

**Cost Risk Allocation Under FFP:**

```
Total Aircraft Cost = Fixed Price to Customer
├─ Vertically Integrated Costs (45% of value)
│  ├─ Labor: Directly controllable via productivity improvements
│  ├─ Materials: Long-term agreements with price certainty
│  └─ Overhead: Amortized over production volume
│
└─ Supplier Procurement (55% of value)
   ├─ Fixed-price subcontracts: Risk transferred, but limited cost-down
   ├─ Cost-plus elements: Margin erosion risk on overruns
   └─ Commercial items: Market price volatility exposure
```

**Key Insight:** Every percentage point of vertical integration **converts variable cost exposure** (supplier price increases, delivery delays, quality escapes) **into controllable internal cost**. Target integration of systems with:
1. **High historical cost growth** (engines: stable; avionics: volatile)
2. **Supply chain concentration risk** (single/dual source items)
3. **Quality/schedule sensitivity** (critical path items)

## 2. VERTICAL INTEGRATION PHILOSOPHY

### 2.1 Guiding Principles

**Principle 1: Own the Differentiation**
> *Vertically integrate systems that create competitive advantage and are difficult for competitors to replicate.*

- **Autonomous GNC System:** Core IP differentiator; in-house software/hardware co-design
- **Tandem Rotor System Integration:** Unique configuration expertise; proprietary dynamic modeling
- **Flight Control Laws:** Safety-critical software; enables rapid certification basis evolution

**Principle 2: Partner on Commodities and Specialties**
> *Buy systems where suppliers have scale advantages or where technology is non-differentiating.*

- **Turboshaft Engines:** Specialized capability; GE/Pratt/Safran have billions in sunk R&D
- **Avionics Processors/Displays:** Commercial standards (ARINC, FACE); supplier ecosystems well-developed
- **Landing Gear:** Mature technology; UTC/Collins/Safran have certified product lines

**Principle 3: Control the Critical Path**
> *Integrate activities that are schedule-critical and prone to supplier delays.*

- **Final Assembly:** Direct control over production flow and customer delivery commitments
- **Systems Integration:** Avoid "integration hell" by owning test/troubleshooting capability
- **Software Integration:** Weekly/daily iteration cycles incompatible with supplier waterfalls

**Principle 4: Invest Where Learning Curves Are Steep**
> *Vertical integration pays dividends where internal learning drives significant cost reduction over production life.*

- **Composite Structures:** 85% learning curve on labor hours (layup, curing, trimming)
- **Wiring Harnesses:** 82% learning curve; design-for-manufacturability improvements
- **Structural Assembly:** 78% learning curve; fixturing/tooling optimization

**Principle 5: Preserve Strategic Flexibility**
> *Maintain optionality for future variants and international partnerships.*

- Own interfaces (ICDs) to enable supplier competition and variant customization
- Modular architecture: "plug-and-play" mission systems for rapid reconfiguration
- Export control: In-house ownership of ITAR systems enables sanitized export variants

### 2.2 Strategic vs. Tactical Integration

| Integration Type | Time Horizon | Decision Authority | Examples |
|-----------------|--------------|-------------------|----------|
| **Strategic** | 10+ years; product lifecycle | Executive Leadership + Board | Autonomy stack, rotor design, final assembly |
| **Tactical** | 1-3 years; production phase | Program Manager + Chief Engineer | Specific machining operations, harness assembly, testing |
| **Opportunistic** | <1 year; market conditions | Supply Chain Director | Supplier consolidation, insourcing bottleneck parts |

**Governance:** Strategic integration decisions require formal **Business Case Analysis** (BCA) with:
- 10-year NPV and IRR modeling
- Make vs. Buy cost crossover analysis
- Risk-adjusted sensitivity cases
- Executive Committee approval for investments >$25M

## 3. MAKE VS BUY DECISION FRAMEWORK

### 3.1 Decision Criteria Matrix

**Weighted Scoring Model** (100-point scale):

| Criterion | Weight | Make Score (High) | Buy Score (High) |
|-----------|--------|------------------|------------------|
| **Strategic Alignment** | 25% | Core competency, differentiating technology | Commodity, non-core |
| **Cost Competitiveness** | 20% | Internal cost < supplier quote (incl. overhead) | Supplier has scale/specialization advantage |
| **Schedule Impact** | 15% | Critical path item; supplier lead time risk | Off critical path; supplier reliable |
| **Quality/Technical Risk** | 15% | Complex integration; proprietary knowledge | Mature technology; supplier certified |
| **IP Protection** | 10% | High IP value; difficult to protect via contract | Low IP sensitivity; open standards |
| **Supply Chain Risk** | 10% | Single/dual source; supplier concentration | Competitive supplier base |
| **Capital Investment** | 5% | Existing capacity or low capex required | High tooling/facilities investment needed |

**Decision Rule:**
- **Make:** Total score ≥60 AND Strategic Alignment ≥18/25
- **Buy:** Total score <60 OR Capital Investment prohibitive (>$50M for single system)
- **Partnership/Co-Development:** Score 50-60 AND high technical complexity

### 3.2 Make vs Buy Decision Process

**Stage 1: Preliminary Screening (PDR -12 months)**
- Identify all systems/assemblies from Drawing Tree (Level 2-3)
- Apply screening filters:
  - ITAR-sensitive? (Flag for Make consideration)
  - Single-source available? (Flag for Make consideration)
  - Annual value <$500K? (Default to Buy unless strategic)
  
**Stage 2: Detailed Analysis (PDR to CDR)**
- Request for Information (RFI) to supplier base
- Internal cost estimation (manufacturing engineering)
- Build vs. Buy Business Case for flagged items
- Technical risk assessment (Chief Engineer review)

**Stage 3: Executive Decision (CDR)**
- Present top 20 systems by value (representing 80% of aircraft cost)
- Formal Make/Buy recommendation with supporting data
- Executive Committee approval and capital allocation
- Supplier selection for "Buy" decisions

**Stage 4: Continuous Review (Production)**
- Annual "Should-Cost" review of supplier pricing
- Opportunity analysis for selective insourcing (bottlenecks, quality issues)
- Quarterly supply chain risk assessment

### 3.3 Special Considerations for FFP Contracts

**FFP-Specific Decision Weights:**

Under FFP contracts, **cost predictability** and **schedule certainty** outweigh **absolute lowest cost**:

| Scenario | Traditional Weight | FFP-Adjusted Weight | Rationale |
|----------|-------------------|-------------------|-----------|
| **Supplier 10% cheaper but 20% schedule risk** | Buy (cost優位) | Make (risk adjusted) | Schedule delay = program penalties + margin erosion |
| **Supplier 5% more expensive, certified process** | Ambiguous | Buy (de-risk quality) | Quality escape costs >>5% in FFP environment |
| **Internal cost competitive at Lot 3+** | Buy early, switch later | Make from Lot 1 | Learning curve predictability > near-term cost |

**Risk-Adjusted Cost Formula:**

```
Adjusted Cost = Base Cost + (Probability of Overrun × Overrun Magnitude) + (Schedule Delay Cost)

Example:
Supplier Quote: $1.0M base
Internal Estimate: $1.1M base (10% higher)

Supplier Risk Adjustment:
  + (30% probability × $400K quality escape) = +$120K
  + (15% probability × 8-week delay × $50K/week) = +$60K
  = $1.18M risk-adjusted

Decision: MAKE ($1.1M < $1.18M risk-adjusted)
```
