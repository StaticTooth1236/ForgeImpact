## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

This Supply Chain & Procurement Plan establishes the strategic framework for acquiring all materials, components, systems, and services required to execute the MAAP-1 program from design validation through full-rate production and sustainment. The plan addresses sourcing strategy, supplier selection and management, procurement execution, risk mitigation, and export compliance for a complex aerospace platform spanning seven production phases over an eight-year horizon.

### Program Scale & Complexity

**Total Program Value:** ~$5.8 billion in procurement spend over lifecycle  
**Unique Part Numbers:** 47,500+ items  
**Direct Suppliers:** 320+ companies across 18 countries  
**Extended Supply Chain:** 1,200+ Tier 2/3 entities  
**Production Span:** 2025–2032 (230+ aircraft across three variants)

### Strategic Objectives

1. **Secure Long-Lead, Critical Path Items:** Propulsion, avionics, and structural forgings with 18–52 month lead times must be committed by Q2 2025 to support first production unit delivery in Q1 2026.

2. **Achieve Cost Competitiveness:** Target 39% unit cost reduction from LRIP-1 to FRP-2 through supplier learning curves, volume leverage, competitive sourcing, and design-to-cost initiatives.

3. **Build Resilient, Scalable Supply Chain:** Capacity must scale 24× from 2 units/year (2025) to 48 units/year (2030) without compromising quality, schedule, or cost performance.

4. **Ensure Export Compliance:** Manage ITAR/EAR-controlled technologies across multinational supply base while enabling NATO/Five-Eyes export variants.

5. **Maintain Schedule Integrity:** >95% supplier on-time delivery (LRIP), >98% (FRP) to support manufacturing ramp and customer commitments.

### Organization of This Document

- **Section 1–3:** Strategy, objectives, and governance framework
- **Section 4–6:** Sourcing philosophy, supplier selection, and management
- **Section 7–10:** Procurement by subsystem, critical items, and contracting approach
- **Section 11–13:** Risk management, export control, and performance metrics
- **Section 14–16:** Integration with IMS/MRP, technology/quality, and closeout

## 1. INTRODUCTION & PROGRAM CONTEXT

### 1.1 Program Overview

The MAAP-1 program represents a next-generation, multi-role aerospace platform incorporating:

- **Tandem-rotor heavy-lift configuration** with autonomous/optionally-piloted capability
- **Three mission variants:** Firefighting (MAAP-1F), ISR (MAAP-1I), Cargo (MAAP-1C)
- **Common core "Green Aircraft"** with ≥80% structural/systems commonality
- **Advanced propulsion:** Dual turboshaft engines (2,200 shp each, FADEC-controlled)
- **Integrated avionics:** Open-architecture mission systems (MOSA-compliant)
- **Composite-intensive airframe:** AS4/8552 carbon fiber, aluminum 7075/7050 primary structure

**Key Performance Parameters (from PRB):**
- Maximum Gross Takeoff Weight: 26,000–28,500 lb
- External Sling Load: 12,000 lb (threshold), 16,000 lb (objective)
- Ferry Range: 450 nm (threshold), 500 nm (objective)
- Hover Ceiling OGE: 8,000 ft PA (ISA+20°C)
- Autonomy Level: Level 3 (supervised autonomous) at IOC, Level 4 goal by FOC

### 1.2 Production Profile & Procurement Horizon

The supply chain must support a seven-phase production ramp as defined in the Manufacturing Ramp Plan:

| Phase | Period | Annual Rate | Cumulative Units | Supply Chain Complexity |
|-------|--------|-------------|------------------|------------------------|
| **LRIP-1** | 2025–2026 | 2/year | 4 | Supplier qualification, first articles |
| **LRIP-2** | 2027 | 6/year | 10 | Process stabilization, dual-sourcing |
| **LRIP-3** | 2028 | 12/year | 22 | Supplier capacity expansion initiation |
| **FRP-1** | 2029 | 24/year | 46 | Full capacity activation |
| **FRP-2** | 2030–2031 | 48/year | 142 | Sustained high-rate operations |
| **Sustain** | 2032+ | 48/year | 230+ | Spares transition, technology refresh |

**Critical Procurement Windows:**
- **Now–Q2 2025:** Long-lead items for LRIP-1 (engines, avionics, landing gear forgings)
- **Q3 2025–Q2 2026:** LRIP-2 materials, supplier capacity commitment
- **2027–2028:** FRP supplier capacity expansion investments ($250M+ externally funded)
- **2029+:** High-rate procurement execution, cost reduction initiatives

## 2. SUPPLY CHAIN STRATEGY & OBJECTIVES

### 2.1 Strategic Vision

**"Build a world-class, resilient, and ethical aerospace supply chain capable of delivering mission-critical systems on-time, on-cost, and with zero defects, while enabling international partnerships and sustainable operations."**

This vision translates into four strategic pillars:

#### Pillar 1: **Supplier Excellence & Partnership**
Move beyond transactional relationships to strategic partnerships with Tier 1 suppliers, characterized by:
- Joint investment in capacity and capability
- Transparent cost/schedule visibility
- Collaborative risk management
- Long-term commitment beyond single program

#### Pillar 2: **Supply Chain Resilience**
Design robustness into the supply base to withstand disruptions:
- Dual-sourcing for high-risk, high-volume items
- Geographic diversification (domestic + allied nation sourcing)
- Financial health monitoring and early intervention
- Buffer inventory strategies for critical path items

#### Pillar 3: **Digital Integration & Visibility**
Leverage Industry 4.0 technologies for real-time supply chain insight:
- Supplier portal with order/delivery/quality dashboards
- Predictive analytics for demand planning and risk detection
- Digital twin of supply chain for scenario planning
- Blockchain for traceability (evaluated for critical items)

#### Pillar 4: **Sustainability & Ethical Sourcing**
Align with corporate ESG commitments and customer expectations:
- Conflict minerals compliance (Section 1502 Dodd-Frank Act)
- Carbon footprint transparency and reduction targets
- Responsible labor practices (supplier code of conduct)
- Circular economy principles (material reuse, recycling)

### 2.2 Strategic Objectives & Metrics

| Objective | Metric | LRIP Target | FRP Target | Measurement Frequency |
|-----------|--------|-------------|------------|----------------------|
| **On-Time Delivery** | % of line items delivered per committed date | ≥95% | ≥98% | Weekly |
| **Supplier Quality** | Defects per million opportunities (DPMO) | <1,000 | <300 | Monthly |
| **Cost Competitiveness** | Unit cost reduction vs LRIP-1 baseline | — | 39% | Per unit/quarterly trend |
| **Supply Chain Lead Time** | Average procurement lead time (weighted) | Baseline | -20% | Quarterly |
| **Supplier Financial Health** | % of critical suppliers in "green" financial status | ≥90% | ≥95% | Quarterly |
| **Dual-Source Coverage** | % of critical items with qualified backup source | 40% | 70% | Annual |
| **Inventory Turns** | Turns per year (purchased material) | 6× | 10× | Monthly |
| **Export Compliance** | Violations or incidents | Zero | Zero | Continuous |

### 2.3 Alignment with Program Cost & Schedule Baselines

**Cost Integration:**
- Procurement spend represents ~62% of total program cost ($3.6B of $5.8B lifecycle)
- Material cost learning curve: 95% cumulative average (supplier negotiations + volume)
- Target contribution to unit cost reduction: $13.6M per aircraft (LRIP-1 to FRP-2)

**Schedule Integration:**
- Supply chain critical path items drive program schedule through LRIP-3
- After FRP-1, manufacturing internal operations become critical path
- Buffer strategies: 15% schedule margin on long-lead items, 8% on medium-lead

**Risk Integration:**
- Supply chain risks comprise 45% of program-level risk exposure (pre-mitigation)
- Top 3 risks: Propulsion capacity, avionics obsolescence, single-source dependencies
- Risk burn-down target: Reduce supply chain risk score by 60% from CDR to FRP gate

## 4. MAKE-OR-BUY ANALYSIS & PHILOSOPHY

### 4.1 Strategic Framework

The make-or-buy decision process balances multiple factors beyond simple cost comparison, including:

**Strategic Considerations:**
- **Core Competency:** Does this capability align with Eurus Systems' long-term strategy?
- **Intellectual Property:** Do we need to control proprietary design or process knowledge?
- **Competitive Differentiation:** Does internal production provide market advantage?

**Operational Considerations:**
- **Cost & Efficiency:** Total cost of ownership (TCO) comparison including overhead, capital
- **Quality Control:** Can we achieve tighter tolerances or better reliability in-house?
- **Capacity & Flexibility:** Do we have internal capacity, or would we need capital investment?

**Risk Considerations:**
- **Supply Continuity:** Is the supplier base stable and competitive, or fragile/monopolistic?
- **Schedule Risk:** Can we control lead times better internally vs external dependencies?
- **Technology Risk:** Are we dependent on rapidly obsolescing technologies or single-source expertise?

### 4.2 Make-or-Buy Decision Criteria

**MAKE Decision Drivers:**

| Criterion | Weighting | Threshold for "MAKE" |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Core Competency Alignment | 25% | High strategic value, differentiating capability |
| IP Protection Need | 20% | Proprietary design, competitive moat |
| Supplier Base Inadequacy | 20% | Single source, unstable market, high risk |
| Quality/Performance Control | 15% | Critical characteristics beyond supplier capability |
| Total Cost Advantage | 10% | >15% TCO savings vs buy (including capital) |
| Capacity Availability | 10% | Existing capacity or low capital investment (<$5M) |

**BUY Decision Drivers:**

| Criterion | Weighting | Threshold for "BUY" |
|-----------|-----------|---------------------|
| Supplier Expertise & Scale | 25% | Mature supplier base, economies of scale |
| Capital Avoidance | 20% | Would require >$10M investment to make |
| Non-Core Activity | 20% | Not strategic differentiator, commodity item |
| Speed to Market | 15% | Supplier has proven capability, faster than internal development |
| Risk Transfer | 10% | Supplier absorbs technology/obsolescence risk |
| Flexibility | 10% | Easier to scale or change suppliers than reconfigure internal ops |

### 4.3 MAAP-1 Make-vs-Buy Decisions (Major Items)

#### **MAKE (Internal Production)**

| Item Category | Rationale | Internal Capability |
|---------------|-----------|---------------------|
| **Final Assembly & Integration** | Core competency, IP protection, quality control | Building 7 assembly lines (7 flows at FRP) |
| **Major Structural Assemblies** | Proprietary design, competitive differentiation | CNC machining, composite layup, assembly jigs |
| **Fuselage Sections (Forward/Center/Aft)** | Integral to aircraft performance, trade secret processes | In-house fabrication & assembly |
| **Wing Subassemblies** | Critical aerodynamic surfaces, tight tolerances | Internal machining & integration |
| **Wiring Harnesses** | Configuration control, rapid design changes | Harness shop (150K linear ft/year capacity) |
| **Flight Test Instrumentation** | Proprietary test methods, reusable across programs | Internal engineering & fabrication |

**Total "MAKE" Content:** ~22% by value, ~35% by labor hours

#### **BUY (External Procurement)**

| Item Category | Rationale | Supplier Strategy |
|---------------|-----------|-------------------|
| **Propulsion Systems (Engines, Transmissions)** | Specialized expertise, capital-intensive, economies of scale | Tier 1 strategic partnership (GE, Pratt & Whitney, or equiv) |
| **Avionics & Mission Systems** | Rapidly evolving technology, supplier R&D investment | MOSA-compliant suppliers, competitive refresh |
| **Landing Gear** | Mature supplier base, proven reliability, certification | Dual-source strategy (Safran, Collins, or equiv) |
| **Rotor Blades & Hubs** | Specialized materials & processes, high tooling cost | Single-source with second-source development plan |
| **Hydraulic/Pneumatic Components** | Commodity items, established standards | Multiple suppliers, competitive bidding |
| **Fasteners, Hardware, Raw Materials** | High-volume commodities, price-sensitive | Vendor-managed inventory, long-term agreements |
| **Composite Raw Materials (Prepreg)** | Supplier economies of scale, material certification | Dual-source (Hexcel, Toray), long-term contracts |
| **Forgings & Castings** | Specialized equipment, low volume doesn't justify internal | Strategic suppliers with capacity commitments |

**Total "BUY" Content:** ~78% by value (including engines ~42%, avionics ~18%, other ~18%)

### 4.4 Make-or-Buy Evolution Over Program Life

**LRIP Phases (2025–2028):**
- Higher "buy" percentage due to supplier NRE amortization and internal capacity constraints
- Focus internal resources on design validation and manufacturing process development
- Accept higher unit costs to preserve capital and schedule

**FRP Phases (2029+):**
- Selective "make" expansions where business case supports (e.g., high-volume machined parts)
- Transition some bought items to licensed production or second-source internal capability
- Competitive re-sourcing of non-strategic items to capture volume discounts

**Sustainment Phase (2032+):**
- Potential "make" insourcing of orphaned items (supplier exits market)
- Strategic "buy" for technology refreshes (obsolescence replacements)

## 5. SUPPLIER SELECTION & QUALIFICATION

### 5.1 Supplier Categorization & Tiering

Suppliers are categorized by strategic importance and performance risk to tailor engagement models:

#### **Tier 1 Strategic Suppliers (32 companies)**

**Definition:** Direct suppliers of major subsystems, long-lead items, or sole-source critical components with program-level impact.

**Examples:**
- Propulsion system integrator (engines, transmissions)
- Avionics suite prime
- Landing gear manufacturer
- Rotor system supplier
- Major composite structure fabricators

**Engagement Model:**
- Executive-level relationship owner (CPO or VP level)
- Quarterly business reviews (QBRs) with joint roadmapping
- Long-term agreements (LTAs) with volume commitments
- Joint investment in capacity expansion, tooling, technology
- Co-location of supplier quality engineers (for highest-risk items)
- Early involvement in design (DFM/DFA collaboration)

**Qualification Requirements:**
- AS9100D or equivalent aerospace quality certification
- Financial health assessment (Dun & Bradstreet rating ≥3A2, or equivalent)
- Demonstrated capability on similar programs (reference checks, audits)
- Capacity assessment and scalability plan to 48 units/year
- Cybersecurity assessment (NIST SP 800-171 for CUI-handling suppliers)
- ITAR registration (if handling export-controlled technical data)

---

#### **Tier 2 Preferred Suppliers (95 companies)**

**Definition:** Direct suppliers of production parts, subassemblies, or services; dual-source candidates for strategic items.

**Examples:**
- Machined structural components
- Electrical/electronic subassemblies
- Hydraulic/pneumatic actuators
- Composite detail parts
- Specialty coatings and treatments

**Engagement Model:**
- Commodity Manager relationship owner
- Monthly or bi-monthly performance reviews
- Standard purchase orders or basic ordering agreements (BOAs)
- Supplier development support (process improvement, quality training)
- Competitive bidding with performance-based re-solicitation

**Qualification Requirements:**
- ISO 9001 minimum; AS9100 preferred
- Demonstrated quality system (process FMEAs, SPC, first article inspection capability)
- Financial stability (credit check, payment history review)
- Capacity to meet rate requirements with defined lead times
- Responsiveness and communication (supplier portal participation)

---

#### **Tier 3 Catalog/Commodity Suppliers (180+ companies)**

**Definition:** Suppliers of standard commercial items, fasteners, raw materials, MRO supplies; low technical risk, high volume.

**Examples:**
- Fasteners (AN/MS standard hardware)
- Raw materials (aluminum plate, bar stock, tubing)
- Consumables (adhesives, sealants, cleaning supplies)
- Cutting tools, shop supplies
- Packaging materials

**Engagement Model:**
- Buyer transactional management
- Automated procurement (ERP-driven POs, vendor-managed inventory for high-runners)
- Competitive bidding or catalog pricing with volume discounts
- Minimal oversight beyond receiving inspection

**Qualification Requirements:**
- Business registration and tax compliance
- Product conformity to specifications (material certs, test reports)
- On-time delivery track record
- Responsive customer service

### 5.2 Supplier Selection Process

#### **Phase 1: Market Research & Sourcing Strategy (4–6 weeks)**

**Activities:**
1. **Requirement Definition:** Engineering releases technical specifications, drawing package, quality requirements (ICD-level interface specs for Tier 1)
2. **Market Analysis:** Identify potential suppliers through:
   - Industry databases (ThomasNet, Kompass, aerospace supplier registries)
   - Trade shows and conferences
   - Supplier recommendations from other programs
   - OEM-approved vendor lists (for engines, avionics)
3. **Sourcing Strategy Development:** Determine:
   - Single-source, dual-source, or competitive multi-source approach
   - Domestic vs international sourcing (ITAR, supply chain resilience considerations)
   - Should-cost model (bottom-up cost estimate for negotiation baseline)
4. **Preliminary Supplier Screening:** Narrow field to 3–5 qualified candidates based on:
   - Technical capability alignment
   - Capacity and scalability
   - Financial health and business continuity
   - Geographic and supply chain risk

---

#### **Phase 2: Request for Proposal (RFP) & Evaluation (6–10 weeks)**

**RFP Package Contents:**
- Statement of Work (SOW) with technical specifications
- Bill of Materials (BOM) or parts list with quantities (by production phase)
- Quality requirements (AS9100, NADCAP, specific process specs)
- Delivery schedule and rate ramp expectations
- Contractual terms and conditions template
- Data rights and IP requirements
- Export control requirements (ITAR/EAR compliance)
- Supplier questionnaire (financial, capacity, past performance)

**Evaluation Criteria (Tier 1 Major Subcontracts):**

| Criterion | Weight | Scoring Method |
|-----------|--------|----------------|
| **Technical Approach** | 35% | Proposal evaluation vs SOW; design maturity; risk mitigation plan |
| **Past Performance** | 20% | Reference checks; similar program experience; quality metrics |
| **Cost/Price** | 25% | Should-cost comparison; cost realism; price reasonableness; basis of estimate |
| **Capacity & Schedule** | 10% | Facility tours; production plan; supplier schedule risk assessment |
| **Management & Organization** | 5% | Program management approach; key personnel qualifications; subcontractor management |
| **Financial Health** | 5% | D&B rating; audited financials review; going-concern assessment |

**Evaluation Process:**
1. **Technical Evaluation:** Cross-functional team (Engineering, Quality, Manufacturing, SC) scores proposals
2. **Site Visits:** Top 2–3 candidates; assess facilities, interview personnel, review processes
3. **Cost Analysis:** Finance and Procurement validate cost proposals, conduct cost/price analysis
4. **Best Value Determination:** Integrated assessment; trade-offs between cost and non-cost factors
5. **Competitive Range:** Discussions with top candidates; request Best and Final Offers (BAFOs)
6. **Source Selection:** Document recommendation; executive approval per authority matrix

**Timeline:** 
- RFP release to proposal receipt: 4–6 weeks
- Evaluation and site visits: 3–4 weeks
- Negotiation and award: 1–2 weeks
- **Total: 8–12 weeks** (longer for complex Tier 1 items)

---

#### **Phase 3: Negotiation & Contract Award (2–4 weeks)**

**Negotiation Objectives:**
- **Price:** Target cost within should-cost ±10%; learning curve commitments for multi-year
- **Terms:** Payment terms (net 30 standard; progress payments for long-lead items); warranty (12–24 months standard)
- **Delivery:** Lead time commitments; schedule flexibility; expedite fees; late delivery penalties
- **Quality:** First article inspection (FAI) requirements; process qualification; defect rate targets; corrective action response time
- **IP & Data Rights:** Technical data package ownership; as-built data delivery; design change rights
- **Risk Allocation:** Liability caps; force majeure; material price escalation clauses

**Contract Award:**
- Contracts Manager executes based on negotiated terms
- Kickoff meeting scheduled within 2 weeks (for Tier 1 suppliers)
- Program Management Office (PMO) notified; IMS updated with supplier delivery dates

### 5.3 New Supplier Qualification & Onboarding

#### **Qualification Activities (Pre-Production)**

**Technical Qualification:**
1. **First Article Inspection (FAI):** Supplier manufactures and inspects first unit per AS9102 requirements; Eurus Quality Engineer witnesses or reviews
2. **Process Validation:** Critical processes (e.g., heat treat, welding, composite cure) demonstrated and documented (process FMEAs, control plans)
3. **Capability Studies:** Statistical process control (SPC) data for critical dimensions; Cpk ≥1.33 target
4. **Tooling Approval:** Production tooling first-article; dimensional validation; tool maintenance plan

**Quality System Assessment:**
1. **Supplier Audit:** On-site audit by Eurus Supplier Quality Engineer; AS9100/ISO 9001 conformance; special process certifications (NADCAP for heat treat, welding, NDT)
2. **Drawing & Specification Review:** Engineering confirms supplier understanding; dimensional tolerances; material certifications; marking/identification
3. **Inspection & Test Plan Approval:** Supplier submits inspection plan; critical characteristics identified; hold points for Eurus witness/approval

**Supply Chain Onboarding:**
1. **Supplier Portal Access:** Training on Eurus supplier portal (order acknowledgment, delivery scheduling, quality notifications)
2. **ERP Integration:** EDI setup for automated PO transmission, ASN (advance ship notice), invoicing (for high-volume suppliers)
3. **Packaging & Shipping Requirements:** Training on Eurus logistics standards; labeling; material handling; transportation modes

  - Warranty: 3,000 hours or 3 years, whichever first

## 8. CRITICAL ITEMS & LONG-LEAD PROCUREMENT

### 8.1 Definition of Critical Items

For the purposes of this procurement plan, **Critical Items** are defined as parts, materials, or systems meeting one or more of the following criteria:

1. **Schedule-Critical:** Item delivery is on the program critical path (zero or negative float in IMS); late delivery directly delays aircraft delivery or program milestones
2. **Single-Source / Sole-Source:** No qualified alternate supplier exists or can be developed within program schedule constraints
3. **Long Lead Time:** Procurement lead time exceeds 26 weeks (6 months), requiring advance commitment before firm production orders
4. **High Technical Risk:** Item involves unproven technology, first-time qualification, or significant certification risk
5. **High Financial Value:** Item represents >5% of aircraft recurring flyaway cost (~$1M+ per aircraft at FRP rates)

|---------------|----------------|-----------|
| **Engines** | 2-unit buffer (1 test, 1 production spare) | Covers supplier delivery variance; enables test program continuity |
| **Transmissions** | 1-unit buffer | Long lead time (40 weeks); critical path mitigation |
| **Landing Gear Forgings** | 3-year forward buy (first order, Q2 2025) | Forging die NRE amortization; locks in supplier capacity |
| **Carbon Fiber Prepreg** | 30-day consignment stock (vendor-managed) | Shelf-life constraint (12 months frozen); JIT delivery risk mitigation |
| **Composite Fuselage Barrels** | Zero buffer (pull system from supplier) | Supplier holds WIP inventory; Eurus takes delivery upon acceptance |
| **Avionics LRUs** | 10% buffer pool (shared across aircraft) | Technology refresh flexibility; covers infant mortality failures |

**Total Buffer Inventory Investment:** ~$18M peak (LRIP-3); reduces to ~$8M at FRP (as production flow stabilizes)

#### **8.3.3 Supplier Capacity Reservation**

For critical suppliers (engines, transmission, major composites):
- **Mechanism:** Long-term agreements (LTAs) with minimum-buy commitments and capacity reservation fees
- **Example:** GE engine LTA commits Eurus to minimum 40 engines over 4 years (LRIP phases); GE reserves production line capacity and commits to delivery windows
- **Cost:** Capacity reservation typically 3–5% premium vs spot pricing; justified by schedule assurance

#### **8.3.4 Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) Analysis**

For high-volume, long-lead commodity items (fasteners, raw materials), balance order frequency vs carrying cost:

**Example: Aluminum 7075-T6 Plate (annual consumption FRP: 45,000 lb)**

- **Order Setup Cost:** $1,200 per order (procurement admin, incoming inspection, logistics)
- **Carrying Cost:** 18% annual (cost of capital, warehouse, obsolescence risk)
- **Material Cost:** $12/lb
- **EOQ Formula:** √(2 × Annual Demand × Order Cost / Carrying Cost per Unit)
- **Calculated EOQ:** ~8,500 lb per order → ~5 orders per year → ~70-day supply per order

**Procurement Strategy:** Quarterly orders (4× per year) with supplier-managed consignment inventory at Eurus facility; balances carrying cost vs order efficiency

## 9. CONTRACTING STRATEGY & APPROACH

### 9.1 Contract Type Selection Philosophy

Contract type selection balances risk allocation between Eurus Systems and suppliers, considering technical maturity, cost predictability, and program phase:

| Phase | Preferred Contract Type | Rationale |
|-------|------------------------|-----------|
| **NRE / Development** | Cost-Plus-Fixed-Fee (CPFF) or Cost-Plus-Award-Fee (CPAF) | Technical uncertainty; design iteration expected; cost risk appropriately borne by buyer |
| **LRIP (Low-Rate Initial Production)** | Fixed-Price-Incentive-Fee (FPIF) or FFP with EPA (Economic Price Adjustment) | Transitional phase; cost targets firming but learning curve uncertainty remains; share cost risk |
| **FRP (Full-Rate Production)** | Firm-Fixed-Price (FFP) | Mature design; cost predictable; supplier bears cost risk; drives efficiency |
| **Long-Term Services** | Time-and-Materials (T&M) with Not-To-Exceed (NTE) cap | Variable scope; appropriate for on-site support, engineering services |

### 9.2 Contract Structures by Commodity

| Commodity / System | NRE Contract Type | LRIP Contract Type | FRP Contract Type | Rationale for Structure |
|--------------------|-------------------|-------------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| **Propulsion (Engines, Transmission)** | CPFF | FPIF → FFP at Lot 3 | FFP with EPA (fuel, material indices) | High NRE; mature technology but custom integration; EPA protects against commodity volatility |
| **Avionics (IMA, Mission Systems)** | CPAF (award fee tied to performance gates) | FPIF | FFP (with tech refresh clauses) | Software-intensive; performance incentives appropriate; obsolescence clauses protect both parties |
| **Structures (Composites, Machining)** | CPFF (tooling), FFP (raw materials) | FPIF (first articles) → FFP | FFP | Tooling risk borne by Eurus (NRE); production costs predictable with learning; FFP drives supplier efficiency |
| **Landing Gear** | CPFF | FPIF | FFP | Certification risk in NRE; production costs firm after qualification |
| **COTS/Catalog Items** | N/A | FFP | FFP | Mature commercial items; no NRE; fixed pricing appropriate |
| **On-Site Engineering Support** | T&M with NTE ceiling | T&M with NTE | T&M with NTE or CPFF (if scope defined) | Variable hours; defined labor rates; NTE protects budget |

### 9.4 Pricing & Cost Management Mechanisms

#### **9.4.1 Learning Curve Pricing (LRIP → FRP)**

For labor-intensive items (composites, assembly, machining), contracts incorporate agreed-upon learning curves (typically 85–92% cumulative average slope):

**Example: Composite Fuselage Barrel**
- LRIP-1 Unit Cost (Lot 1, Units 1-4): $285K (baseline)
- Assumed Learning: 85% cumulative average
- FRP-2 Unit Cost (Cumulative Unit 100+): $175K (cost reduced by 39% vs LRIP-1)

**Contract Mechanism:**
- Supplier provides detailed Basis of Estimate (BOE) for LRIP-1 pricing, broken down by labor hours, material, overhead
- Learning curve applied to labor and variable overhead elements (not material cost)
- Pricing for each subsequent lot negotiated based on cumulative quantity and learning formula
- Actual costs tracked; if supplier exceeds learning targets, Eurus may renegotiate or compete for subsequent lots

#### **9.4.2 Economic Price Adjustment (EPA) Clauses**

For long-term contracts (3+ years) and commodities subject to market volatility, EPA clauses adjust pricing based on published indices:

**Example: Engine Contract EPA**
- **Base Price:** $1,425K per engine (LRIP-1 baseline)
- **Adjustment Formula:** 
  - 60% of price tied to labor index (Bureau of Labor Statistics, Aerospace Manufacturing)
  - 25% tied to nickel commodity index (for turbine superalloy materials)
  - 15% fixed (profit, tooling amortization)
- **Adjustment Frequency:** Annual, based on prior 12-month average indices
- **Cap:** ±10% maximum adjustment per year to limit volatility

**Rationale:** Protects both parties from unforeseeable economic changes (inflation, commodity spikes); ensures supplier remains economically viable without windfall profits

#### **9.4.3 Cost Reduction Incentives**

**Shared Savings Programs:**
- Supplier-initiated cost reduction ideas (design changes, process improvements, material substitutions) that achieve measurable savings are eligible for shared savings
- **Split:** 50/50 for first-year savings; 60/40 (Eurus/Supplier) for recurring savings
- **Example:** Supplier proposes alternate fastener that reduces installation time by 15% and costs 8% less → Annual savings $450K → Supplier receives $225K first year, $180K/year thereafter
- **Administration:** Documented via Value Engineering Change Proposal (VECP); approved by Engineering and Supply Chain

**Performance-Based Incentives (FPIF Contracts):**
- Target Cost, Target Profit, Ceiling Price established
- If supplier delivers under target cost, shares savings (e.g., 50/50 split)
- If over target cost, shares overrun up to ceiling price; no profit beyond ceiling

1. Working-level resolution (Buyer ↔ Supplier Account Manager)
2. Management review (Commodity Manager ↔ Supplier Ops Manager)
3. Executive mediation (SC Director ↔ Supplier VP)
4. Legal/Contractual (CPO + Legal ↔ Supplier Executive + Legal)

**Formal Dispute Resolution Mechanisms (Incorporated in Standard T&Cs):**

**Negotiation (First Resort):**
- Good-faith negotiations between authorized representatives
- Documented in writing; 30-day negotiation window

**Mediation (If Negotiation Fails):**
- Neutral third-party mediator (e.g., JAMS, AAA Commercial Mediation)
- Non-binding; costs split 50/50
- 60-day mediation window

**Arbitration (If Mediation Fails):**
- Binding arbitration per American Arbitration Association (AAA) Commercial Arbitration Rules
- Single arbitrator for disputes <$1M; three-arbitrator panel for >$1M
- Venue: [Location specified in contract, typically Eurus headquarters jurisdiction]
- Costs: Each party bears own legal costs; arbitrator fees split unless arbitrator awards otherwise
- Arbitration award is final and binding; enforceable in court

**Litigation (Last Resort):**
- Preserved right to litigate if arbitration clause waived or inapplicable
- Jurisdiction and venue per contract terms (typically Eurus headquarters state/federal court)

**Preference:** Disputes are rare in well-managed supply chains; emphasis on prevention through clear contracts, regular communication, and collaborative problem-solving.

### 10.2 Risk Categories & Taxonomy

Supply chain risks are categorized using a standard taxonomy for consistent identification and reporting:

| Risk Category | Description | Examples |
|---------------|-------------|----------|
| **Supplier Performance** | Risks related to supplier inability to meet quality, delivery, or cost commitments | Late deliveries, quality defects, cost overruns, supplier financial distress |
| **Technical / Design** | Risks related to technical feasibility, design maturity, or integration challenges | First-time design failures, requirement changes impacting supplier, certification delays |
| **Capacity / Scalability** | Risks related to supplier ability to scale production to meet program ramp | Insufficient production capacity, workforce shortages, capital equipment constraints |
| **Single-Source / Dependency** | Risks related to lack of alternate suppliers or supply chain bottlenecks | Sole-source items, proprietary technology, limited supplier base |
| **Geopolitical / External** | Risks external to supplier control (natural disasters, trade restrictions, pandemics) | Export control changes, tariffs, supply chain disruptions (e.g., port strikes), force majeure events |
| **Technology Obsolescence** | Risks related to component end-of-life or technology becoming unavailable | Electronics obsolescence, supplier discontinuing product lines |
| **Cost / Economic** | Risks related to cost volatility, inflation, or economic downturns affecting suppliers | Commodity price spikes, currency fluctuations (for international suppliers), supplier bankruptcy |
| **Quality / Certification** | Risks related to quality system failures or certification delays | AS9100 decertification, FAA/EASA audit findings, counterfeit parts |
| **Cybersecurity / IP** | Risks related to data breaches, IP theft, or cyber attacks on suppliers | Ransomware attacks disrupting supplier operations, IP theft of proprietary designs |

### 10.3 Risk Assessment Methodology

Each identified risk is assessed using a standard 5×5 Risk Matrix:

**Likelihood Scale (1–5):**
1. **Rare:** <10% probability of occurrence
2. **Unlikely:** 10–30% probability
3. **Possible:** 30–50% probability
4. **Likely:** 50–70% probability
5. **Almost Certain:** >70% probability

**Consequence/Impact Scale (1–5):**
1. **Negligible:** Minimal impact; <$100K cost impact, <1 week schedule impact
2. **Minor:** Modest impact; $100K–$500K cost, 1–4 weeks schedule
3. **Moderate:** Significant impact; $500K–$2M cost, 1–2 months schedule, quality issue requiring rework
4. **Major:** Severe impact; $2M–$10M cost, 2–6 months schedule, safety issue, program milestone at risk
5. **Catastrophic:** Program-threatening; >$10M cost, >6 months schedule, loss of aircraft, loss of life

**Risk Score = Likelihood × Consequence** (Range: 1–25)

**Risk Severity Thresholds:**
- **Low Risk (Green):** Score 1–6 → Accept and monitor
- **Medium Risk (Yellow):** Score 7–15 → Mitigation plan required; active management
- **High Risk (Red):** Score 16–25 → Immediate mitigation; executive escalation; may require contract change, alternate sourcing, or design change

#### **10.5.4 Single-Source/Dependency Risk Mitigation**

**Tier 1: Accept Single-Source with Enhanced Protections**
- **Conditions:** No technical alternative exists; development cost/schedule for second source prohibitive; supplier has strong financial health and track record

**Tier 2: Develop Second Source**
- **Triggers:** Item is critical path, high value (>$1M/aircraft), or supplier financial health questionable
- **Timeline:** Typically 18–36 months for second-source qualification
- **Investment:** $5M–$15M per major item (Eurus-funded NRE for second qualification)
- **Examples:** Engines, landing gear, rotor blades (per Section 7)

**Tier 3: Redesign for Multi-Source**
- **Last Resort:** If single-source risk cannot be mitigated and supplier leverage is unacceptable
- **Example:** Avionics selected for MOSA compliance specifically to avoid vendor lock-in (Section 7.3.1)

---

#### **10.5.5 Geopolitical/External Risk Mitigation**

**Supply Chain Diversification:**
- **Geographic Strategy:** 
  - Tier 1 suppliers: Prefer domestic (US) or NATO/Five-Eyes nations
  - Tier 2/3: Accept international suppliers if dual-sourced or non-critical path
  - **China Exclusion Policy:** No direct China-sourced content for ITAR-controlled aircraft; commercial-off-the-shelf (COTS) electronics screened for China manufacturing origin
- **Conflict Minerals Compliance:** Section 1502 Dodd-Frank Act; suppliers certify conflict-free sourcing (3TG: tin, tantalum, tungsten, gold)

**Trade Compliance:**
- **ITAR/EAR Management:** All suppliers handling controlled technical data registered and compliant (Section 11)
- **Tariff Exposure:** Contracts with international suppliers include tariff escalation clauses; ongoing monitoring of Section 301/232 tariffs
- **Force Majeure Clauses:** Standard contract terms address natural disasters, pandemics, government actions; allocation of risk and relief provisions

**Pandemic/Disruption Preparedness:**
- **Lessons from COVID-19:** Supplier business continuity plans (BCPs) reviewed during qualification
- **Pandemic Response Protocol:** 
  - Tier 1 suppliers required to maintain 30-day safety stock of critical sub-tier components
  - Alternative logistics routes pre-identified (air freight vs. ocean freight vs. domestic trucking)
  - Remote quality inspection protocols (video audit capability) for travel restrictions

---

#### **10.5.6 Technology Obsolescence Risk Mitigation**

**Obsolescence Management Strategy (Electronics Focus):**

**Proactive:**
- **MOSA Architecture:** Open-architecture avionics (Section 7.3.1) designed for modular component replacement without aircraft-level redesign
- **Lifecycle Forecasting:** Electronics suppliers provide 10-year production commitment or technology roadmap; end-of-life (EOL) notifications contractually required 24 months in advance
- **Design for Sustainability:** Where feasible, use long-lifecycle components (industrial/automotive-grade vs. consumer-grade electronics); avoid bleeding-edge technology in baseline design

**Reactive:**
- **Lifetime Buys:** Strategic procurement of long-lead-time ICs and components when EOL announced; quantity sized for remaining production + 10 years spares
  - **Example:** If critical flight computer chip discontinued at LRIP-3, purchase 500-unit lot (covers 230 aircraft production + spares pool)
  - **Cost:** Typically $2M–$8M per lifetime buy; budgeted in program reserves
- **Form/Fit/Function Replacement:** Engineering change to substitute equivalent components; requires qualification testing but avoids full redesign
- **Emulation/FPGA Solutions:** Field-Programmable Gate Arrays (FPGAs) can emulate obsolete ASICs; last-resort solution for critical, irreplaceable components

**Technology Refresh Plan:**
- **Avionics:** Planned 5-year refresh cycle (Section 7.3.1); Gen-2 processors at FRP-1, Gen-3 by sustainment phase
- **Mission Systems:** ISR sensors on 5-year technology insertion cycle (Section 7.3.2)
- **Contract Structure:** Pre-negotiated technology refresh pricing in Tier 1 avionics contracts; supplier provides upgrade path at fixed percentage of original unit cost (e.g., 60% of original price for mid-life upgrade)

#### **10.5.7 Cost/Economic Risk Mitigation**

**Material Cost Volatility:**
- **Hedging Strategies:**
  - **Fixed-Price Contracts:** Multi-year agreements lock in pricing for commodity items (aluminum, fasteners)
  - **Economic Price Adjustment (EPA) Clauses:** For long-term contracts, index-based adjustments (Section 9.4.2); protects both parties from extreme volatility
  - **Commodity Futures:** Evaluated for carbon fiber prepreg (high price volatility); pilot program at FRP gate if cost-effective
- **Dual-Source Competition:** Price competition between qualified suppliers (landing gear, actuators, machining); annual re-compete for 20–30% of volume drives cost discipline

**Supplier Financial Health:**
- **Monitoring Frequency:**
  - **Tier 1 Strategic Suppliers:** Quarterly D&B rating checks, annual audited financials review
  - **Tier 2 Preferred Suppliers:** Semi-annual D&B checks
  - **Tier 3 Commodity Suppliers:** Annual review or event-triggered (e.g., bankruptcy news)
- **Red Flag Triggers:**
  - D&B rating drops below 3A2 (or equivalent)
  - Negative equity or going-concern opinion in audited financials
  - Payment default to sub-tier suppliers (detected via industry intelligence)
  - Layoffs or facility closures announced
- **Intervention Actions:**
  - **Green Status:** Continue monitoring
  - **Yellow Status:** Increase payment terms to net-15 (faster cash infusion); activate second source (if exists); request financial recovery plan
  - **Red Status:** Withhold new orders; execute contingency plan (alternate sourcing, production transfer via escrowed tooling); consider supplier bailout (advance payments) if strategically critical and no alternative

**Currency Risk (International Suppliers):**
- Contracts denominated in USD where possible
- For Euro/GBP/CAD suppliers: Currency hedging via forward contracts or natural hedging (revenues in same currency offset costs)

#### **10.5.8 Quality/Certification Risk Mitigation**

**Quality System Oversight:**
- **Supplier Audits:** 
  - **Tier 1:** Annual AS9100 surveillance audits by Eurus Quality team; coordinate with third-party registrar audits
  - **Tier 2:** Biennial audits or event-driven (quality escape, customer complaint)
  - **Special Process Audits:** NADCAP-certified processes (heat treat, welding, NDT, chemical processing) audited at qualification and every 2 years
- **First Article Inspection (FAI):** Mandatory per AS9102 for all production parts; no lot acceptance without FAI approval (Section 5.3)
- **In-Process Inspection:** Witness/hold points defined in inspection plans for critical characteristics; Eurus Quality Engineer sign-off required before next operation

**Counterfeit Parts Prevention:**
- **AS6081 / AS5553 Compliance:** Suppliers required to source electronic components only from OEMs or authorized distributors; chain of custody documentation
- **Incoming Inspection:** 100% inspection of high-risk items (electronics, raw materials) for authenticity; use of X-ray, decapsulation, and electrical testing for suspect parts
- **Traceability:** Lot traceability to raw material heat lot for all primary structure materials; QR code/serialization for major assemblies

**Non-Conformance Management:**
- **Supplier Notification:** 24-hour notification requirement for non-conforming material discovered at supplier
- **Material Review Board (MRB):** Joint Eurus-supplier MRB for disposition (use-as-is, rework, scrap, return-to-supplier)
- **Root Cause Analysis:** 8D methodology required for all major non-conformances (DPMO >1000, safety impact, schedule impact >1 week)
- **Corrective Action Verification:** Eurus Quality Engineer verifies corrective action implementation before lot release

## 11. EXPORT CONTROL & TRUSTED SUPPLIER REQUIREMENTS

### 11.1 Regulatory Framework

The MAAP-1 program involves technologies and technical data controlled under:
- **International Traffic in Arms Regulations (ITAR):** 22 CFR §120-130, administered by U.S. Department of State, Directorate of Defense Trade Controls (DDTC)
  - **USML Category VIII(h):** Military aircraft (rotorcraft exceeding specified performance)
  - **USML Category XI(a):** Military electronics (fire control, autonomous navigation)
- **Export Administration Regulations (EAR):** 15 CFR §730-774, administered by U.S. Department of Commerce, Bureau of Industry and Security (BIS)
  - **ECCN 9A610:** Military aircraft not controlled under USML Category VIII (export variants with reduced capability)
  - **ECCN 3A001/4A003:** Certain avionics and electronics subject to dual-use controls

**Eurus Systems ITAR Registration:** K-12345 (current through December 2026)

---

### 11.2 Technology Control Plan (TCP)

A comprehensive Technology Control Plan (TCP-MAAP1-001, current revision C) governs all export-controlled aspects of the MAAP-1 program. The TCP is reviewed and updated at each program phase gate and approved by the Export Compliance Manager and Program Manager.

**TCP Key Elements:**
1. **USML Categorization:** All MAAP-1 hardware, software, and technical data classified by USML/ECCN category
2. **Supplier Registration Verification:** All suppliers handling ITAR-controlled technical data verified for current DDTC registration
3. **Foreign Person Access Controls:** Procedures for Technology Control Plans (TCPs) when foreign persons (including permanent residents) access controlled data
4. **Export Licensing Strategy:** Pre-determined licensing approach for anticipated FMS customers (NATO, Five-Eyes, Major Non-NATO Allies)
5. **Technical Data Segregation:** Architecture for separating ITAR-controlled design data from exportable data (enables coalition sales without full technical data release)

---

### 11.3 Supplier Export Compliance Requirements

#### **11.3.1 ITAR-Registered Suppliers (Tier 1 & Select Tier 2)**

**Mandatory Requirements:**
- **DDTC Registration:** Current and valid ITAR registration; Eurus verifies via DDTC public portal quarterly
- **Technology Control Plan (Supplier TCP):** Suppliers receiving ITAR technical data must maintain an approved TCP; Eurus audits during qualification and annually thereafter
- **Foreign National Access Controls:** Suppliers must screen employees for U.S. Person status before granting access to ITAR data; foreign persons require export license (typically DSP-5 for permanent employment)
- **Secure Data Handling:** 
  - Technical data marked with ITAR distribution statement (e.g., "Export Controlled — ITAR 22 CFR §120-130")
  - Electronic data transmitted via encrypted channels (minimum AES-256)
  - Physical data stored in controlled access areas (badge access, visitor logs)
- **Re-Export Restrictions:** Suppliers prohibited from re-exporting ITAR-controlled items or data without Eurus and DDTC authorization
- **Audit Rights:** Eurus and U.S. Government (DDTC, DCSA) reserve right to audit supplier export compliance program

**Supplier Training:**
- ITAR awareness training provided by Eurus at contract kickoff (web-based module, 90 minutes)
- Annual refresher training required
- Supplier compliance officer designated as point of contact for export matters

---

#### **11.3.2 Non-ITAR Suppliers (Tier 3 & Commodity)**

**Requirements:**
- **EAR Compliance:** If supplier receives EAR-controlled technical data (e.g., drawings for commercial-grade components), must comply with EAR restrictions (no export to embargoed countries, sanctioned parties)
- **Deemed Export Awareness:** Suppliers made aware that sharing technical data with foreign nationals may constitute a "deemed export" requiring authorization
- **Screening Against Denied Parties Lists:** Eurus screens all suppliers (ITAR and non-ITAR) against:
  - DDTC Debarred List
  - BIS Denied Persons List / Entity List
  - OFAC Specially Designated Nationals (SDN) List
  - Consolidated Screening List
  - **Frequency:** At contract award and quarterly thereafter

### 11.4 Export Variant Strategy

To enable international sales without full technical data release and to accommodate customers without access to U.S.-controlled technologies, the program includes defined **export variants**:

#### **11.4.1 MAAP-1C Export (Cargo Variant)**

**Baseline vs. Export Differences:**

| System | Domestic Baseline | Export Variant | USML/ECCN Impact |
|--------|------------------|----------------|------------------|
| **Autonomous GNC** | Level 3 autonomy (supervised autonomous) | Level 2 (optionally-piloted only); autonomous functions disabled or removed | Reduces USML XI(a) sensitivity; shifts some functionality to ECCN 7D003 (navigation software) |
| **GPS/INS** | M-code military GPS receiver | Commercial P(Y)-code GPS | Removes USML restriction; becomes ECCN 7A003 |

| **Datalink** | Link 16 (MIDS-JTRS) | Omitted or replaced with commercial SATCOM-only | Removes USML XI(c) tactical datalink restriction |
| **Defensive Systems** | Missile warning sensors | Omitted (not included in Cargo baseline; customer option) | N/A for baseline export Cargo variant |
| **Engine** | Domestic turboshaft (GE T700 or equiv) | FAA-certified commercial derivative (e.g., GE CT7) with equivalent power | Removes USML VIII(h) military-specific engine restriction |

**Technical Data Release:**
- **Operational/Maintenance Data:** Released to export customers (flight manuals, maintenance manuals, illustrated parts catalogs)

### 12.3 Procurement Lead Time vs. Manufacturing Cycle Time

**Manufacturing Cycle Time (Final Assembly):**
- **LRIP-1 (Aircraft #1–#4):** 180 days (6 months) from first structural assembly to flight test delivery
  - Learning curve steep; first article quality issues; limited tooling/workforce
- **LRIP-2 (Aircraft #5–#10):** 120 days (4 months)
  - Process stabilization; workforce learning; tooling optimization
- **LRIP-3 (Aircraft #11–#22):** 75 days (2.5 months)
  - Flow-line assembly begins; improved productivity
- **FRP-1 (Aircraft #23–#46):** 45 days (1.5 months)
  - Full flow-line; 7 assembly positions; throughput-optimized
- **FRP-2 (Aircraft #47+):** 30 days (1 month) at peak rate (48 aircraft/year = 1 aircraft every 7.5 days entering final assembly; 30-day flow time)

**Implication for Procurement:**
- Long-lead items (engines 52 weeks, transmission 40 weeks) must be ordered well before manufacturing cycle time
- **LRIP-1:** Order engines 12 months before manufacturing start (18 months before flight test delivery)
- **FRP-2:** Order engines 12 months before assembly start (13 months before delivery); manufacturing cycle time is now only 1 month

**Procurement-to-Manufacturing Handoff:**
- **Kitting Strategy:** All material for an aircraft "kit" staged in manufacturing before assembly start
  - **Kit Complete Date:** Target 2 weeks before assembly start
  - **Kit Shortage Meeting:** Weekly review of upcoming aircraft kits; identify shortages and expedite actions
- **Phased Deliveries:** For large assemblies (fuselage sections), deliveries sequenced to match assembly flow
  - Example: Forward fuselage delivered Week 1, center fuselage Week 3, aft fuselage Week 4 of a 12-week assembly flow

### 12.4 Manufacturing Ramp Risk & Supply Chain Flexibility

**Ramp Risk:** The 24× production increase from LRIP-1 (2 aircraft/year) to FRP-2 (48 aircraft/year) over 5 years represents significant risk to both manufacturing and supply chain.

**Supply Chain Flexibility Mechanisms:**

**1. Supplier Capacity Ramp Gates:**
- Formal supplier capacity reviews at each program phase gate (LRIP-2, LRIP-3, FRP-1)
- **Go/No-Go Decision:** Supplier must demonstrate capacity readiness (equipment, workforce, cash flow) before Eurus authorizes production rate increase
- If supplier not ready: Hold rate increase, activate alternate source, or provide Eurus-funded capacity support

**2. Rate-Reduction Options:**
- Contracts include provisions for Eurus to reduce order quantities by up to 25% without penalty (to accommodate program delays or demand changes)
- Supplier receives termination liability for unrecoverable costs (tooling, long-lead materials ordered at Eurus direction)

**3. Rate-Increase Options:**
- Contracts include options for Eurus to increase quantities by up to 50% with 12-month notice
- Pricing for optional quantities negotiated upfront; supplier commits to capacity expansion plan if options exercised

**4. Flexible Delivery Scheduling:**
- Eurus provides 18-month firm forecast, 36-month planning forecast to suppliers
- Firm window: Supplier must deliver per committed dates (liquidated damages apply)
- Planning window: Forecast only; adjustments allowed with 12-month notice

### 13.3 Continuous Improvement Programs

#### **13.3.1 Supplier Cost Reduction Initiatives**

**Target:** 3–5% annual cost reduction from LRIP-2 through FRP-2 (per Section 2.2, Strategic Objective SO-3)

**Mechanisms:**

**Value Engineering (VE) Workshops:**
- Conducted annually with strategic suppliers (Tier 1)

- **Tool:** Parametric cost models (cost estimating relationships based on part complexity, material, processes)

**Competitive Re-Sourcing:**
- Non-strategic items (Tier 2/3) re-competed annually or bi-annually
- 20–30% of portfolio re-bid per year to maintain competitive pressure
- Incumbent supplier given opportunity to match or beat competitive pricing

**Learning Curve Management:**
- Track actual supplier labor hours vs. negotiated learning curve (typically 85–92% slope)
- If supplier outperforms learning curve (due to automation, process improvement), negotiate accelerated cost reduction
- If supplier underperforms, trigger root cause analysis and corrective action

**Annual Savings Target:**
- **LRIP Phase:** $500K–$1M annual savings (primarily VE and should-cost negotiations)

### 14.5 Cybersecurity for Supply Chain Systems

**Threat Landscape:**
- Supply chain systems are high-value targets for cyberattacks (ransomware, data theft, IP exfiltration)
- Supplier portals are externally accessible → attack surface

**Security Measures:**

**1. Network Segmentation:**
- Supply chain systems (ERP, supplier portal) on separate network segment from engineering systems (PLM, design servers)
- Firewalls and intrusion detection/prevention systems (IDS/IPS) monitor traffic

**2. Access Controls:**
- Multi-factor authentication (MFA) required for all supplier portal access
- Role-based access control (RBAC) in ERP: buyers can create POs, cannot approve; managers approve POs, cannot edit master data
- Privileged access management (PAM) for system administrators

**3. Data Encryption:**
- Data at rest: AES-256 encryption for databases (ERP, QMS, supplier portal)
- Data in transit: TLS 1.2+ for all web traffic; VPN for supplier file transfers (technical drawings, test data)

**4. Vulnerability Management:**
- Quarterly vulnerability scans of supply chain systems; critical vulnerabilities patched within 30 days
- Annual penetration testing of supplier portal by third-party ethical hackers

**5. Incident Response:**
- Supply chain cyberattack scenario included in Eurus Incident Response Plan (IRP-0001)
- Supplier portal compromise scenario: Isolate portal, notify suppliers via alternate channel, forensic investigation, restore from clean backup
- Recovery time objective (RTO): <24 hours for supplier portal; <8 hours for ERP

**6. Supplier Cybersecurity (Section 11.6):**
- NIST SP 800-171 compliance for suppliers handling CUI
- Supplier cyber incidents reported to Eurus within 72 hours per contract terms

### 15.2 Supplier Quality Requirements

---

#### **15.2.2 First Article Inspection (FAI)**

**Requirement:** All production parts require FAI per AS9102 before lot acceptance (applies to Tier 1, Tier 2, and critical Tier 3 items).

**FAI Process:**
1. **Supplier Preparation:** Supplier manufactures first article using production tooling, processes, and personnel
2. **Full Dimensional Inspection:** Supplier inspects 100% of drawing characteristics; records results on AS9102 Form 3
3. **Material Certification:** Supplier provides material certs (chemical composition, mechanical properties, heat lot traceability)
4. **Special Process Certification:** Supplier provides evidence of special process compliance (heat treat charts, weld procedure qualification records, NDT reports)
5. **FAI Package Submission:** Supplier submits complete FAI package (Form 1: Index, Form 2: Product Accountability, Form 3: Characteristics Accountability, supporting data) to Eurus Quality
6. **Eurus Review:** Supplier Quality Engineer (SQE) reviews FAI package for completeness and compliance; may witness inspection at supplier facility (required for critical/complex parts)
7. **FAI Approval:** SQE approves FAI; part released for production lot acceptance
8. **FAI Database:** Approved FAI packages stored in QMS database; accessible for future reference (e.g., if supplier changes process, new FAI required)

**Timeline:** FAI review and approval target: 10 business days from submission (critical path items expedited to 5 days)

---

#### **15.2.3 Receiving Inspection**

**Scope:** All incoming purchased materials and components subject to receiving inspection prior to acceptance and use in production.

**Inspection Levels:**

| Level | Description | When Applied |
|-------|-------------|--------------|
| **Level 1: Documentation Review** | Review packing slip, material certs, CoC (Certificate of Conformance); no physical inspection | COTS items from established suppliers with good quality history (Green scorecard status) |
| **Level 2: Sample Inspection** | Visual inspection + dimensional check of sample (10% of lot or 3 pieces, whichever greater) | Standard production parts from qualified suppliers |
| **Level 3: 100% Inspection** | Full dimensional inspection of every piece in lot | First article, critical safety items, new suppliers, suppliers with quality history (Yellow/Red scorecard) |
| **Level 4: Source Inspection** | Eurus SQE inspects at supplier facility before shipment (Eurus acceptance at source) | Long-lead, high-value, complex assemblies (engines, transmissions, landing gear) |

**Non-Conforming Material:**
- Material failing receiving inspection quarantined (red-tagged, segregated area)
- Material Review Board (MRB) dispositions non-conformance: Use-As-Is, Rework, Return-to-Supplier, Scrap
- Supplier issued Supplier Corrective Action Request (SCAR); root cause analysis and corrective action required (8D report within 10 business days)

**Inspection Equipment:**
- Coordinate Measuring Machines (CMM) for complex geometry
- Optical comparators, micrometers, calipers for standard dimensions
- Material test lab (tensile testing, hardness testing, chemical analysis) for material verification
- Eurus-owned equipment maintained per ISO 10012 (calibration every 6–12 months; NIST-traceable)

- **Scope:** Deep-dive root cause investigation; may include sub-tier supplier audits if issue originates there

- Eurus SQE may participate in NADCAP audit as observer
- Suppliers must maintain current NADCAP accreditation; lapse results in suspension of special process work until re-accreditation

### 15.4 Corrective Action & Continuous Improvement (CAPA)

**Supplier Corrective Action Request (SCAR) Process:**

**Trigger:** Non-conforming material, delivery issue, quality system deficiency identified during audit, or customer complaint

**SCAR Workflow:**
1. **SCAR Issuance:** Eurus Quality issues SCAR to supplier via QMS portal; includes description of issue, supporting evidence (photos, inspection reports, customer complaint), required response date (typically 10 business days for root cause, 30 days for corrective action implementation)
2. **Supplier Acknowledgment:** Supplier acknowledges SCAR receipt within 24 hours
3. **Root Cause Analysis:** Supplier conducts root cause analysis using 8D (Eight Disciplines) or equivalent methodology:
   - **D1:** Form team
   - **D2:** Describe problem
   - **D3:** Implement containment actions (prevent further non-conforming material from shipping)
   - **D4:** Identify root cause (5-Why, Fishbone, Fault Tree Analysis)
   - **D5:** Choose corrective actions
   - **D6:** Implement corrective actions
   - **D7:** Prevent recurrence (update procedures, training, process controls)
   - **D8:** Congratulate team
4. **Eurus Review:** SQE reviews 8D report; approves corrective action plan or requests additional information/clarification
5. **Verification:** SQE verifies corrective action effectiveness (may require on-site verification visit, review of inspection data, or submission of revised FAI)
6. **SCAR Closure:** Once verified effective, SCAR closed in QMS; remains in supplier's quality history for future reference

**SCAR Escalation:**
- **Minor SCAR:** Isolated non-conformance; corrective action straightforward; closed within 30 days → No impact to supplier scorecard if rare occurrence
- **Major SCAR:** Repeat issue, systemic quality system failure, safety impact, or delivery impact → Supplier scorecard downgraded to Yellow or Red; may trigger for-cause audit
- **Chronic Issues:** Supplier with ≥3 major SCARs in 12 months → Executive escalation; supplier placed on probation (Section 15.5)

**Preventive Action (PA):**
- Eurus encourages suppliers to identify and address potential issues before they result in non-conformances (proactive mindset)
- Suppliers may initiate Preventive Action Requests (PARs) for process improvements, risk mitigations, or lessons learned from other programs
- Eurus recognizes suppliers with active PA programs in annual Supplier Excellence Awards

---
