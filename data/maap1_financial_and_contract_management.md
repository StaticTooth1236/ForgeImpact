## EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

### Program Financial Overview

The MAAP-1 program operates under a **Firm Fixed Price (FFP)** contract structure with defined milestone payments, performance incentives, and contractual liquidated damages. This document establishes the financial management framework designed to protect program margin, optimize cash flow, and manage technical and schedule risk within the FFP construct.

**Key Financial Parameters:**

| Metric | Value | Notes |
|--------|-------|-------|
| **Total Contract Value** | $4,128M | FFP with economic price adjustments |
| **Total Program Cost (EAC)** | $3,756M | Includes risk reserve |
| **Target Gross Margin** | 9.9% | Pre-tax, before G&A allocation |
| **Program Duration** | 132 months | Jan 2025 – Dec 2035 |
| **Development Phase** | 48 months | Through First Flight + Qualification |
| **Production Phase** | 84 months | LRIP through FRP sustainment |
| **Total Units Delivered** | 192 | 3 DTA, 6 FTA, 183 production |

**Contract Structure:**
- **Prime Contract:** FFP Development + FFP Production Lots 1-9
- **Payment Structure:** Milestone-based with monthly progress payments
- **Economic Price Adjustments:** Labor and material indices per contract clause
- **Performance Incentives:** $45M available for early delivery and quality
- **Liquidated Damages:** $2M per month late delivery penalty after 90-day grace

**Financial Management Philosophy:**

This program adopts a **margin-first financial discipline** consistent with modern defense contractors (Anduril, Palantir, Shield AI approach) rather than traditional EVMS-heavy cost-plus structures:

1. **Margin Protection:** Proactive risk identification and reserve management
2. **Cash Discipline:** Optimize working capital and minimize Days Sales Outstanding (DSO)
3. **Risk Quantification:** Probabilistic cost modeling with management reserve strategy
4. **Change Control Rigor:** All engineering changes evaluated for margin impact
5. **Supplier Management:** Cost and schedule performance directly tied to program margin
6. **Simplified Metrics:** VP-level KPIs focused on margin, cash, and risk burn rate

### 3.3 Margin Protection Strategy

**Tier 1: Cost Baseline Protection**
- Rigorous cost estimating with independent validation
- Conservative learning curve assumptions (85% vs industry 82%)

- $74M management reserve (1.5% of contract value)

- Executive approval required for reserve usage >$1M

- All engineering changes evaluated for cost/schedule/margin impact
- Class I changes require customer funding (no internal absorption)

**Tier 4: Supplier Cost Management**
- Should-cost modeling for all major procurements
- Competitive bidding for non-sole-source items
- Annual supplier cost reduction targets (3% average)
- Supplier performance tied to payment terms and future awards

**Tier 5: Production Efficiency**
- Lean manufacturing implementation (5% productivity gain target)
- Automation investment ROI: >20% over program life
- Continuous improvement culture with team incentives
- Learning curve tracking and corrective action if off-plan

- Reserve usage approved by Program VP (>$500K) or CFO (>$2M)

**Quarterly Risk Deep-Dive:**
- Cross-functional team reviews entire risk register (typically 80-120 active risks)
- Reserve adequacy assessment (Monte Carlo re-run)

- Reserve replenishment from opportunities (if margin improvement realized)

**Target:** Reserve depletes linearly as risks retire through program phases.  
**Red Flag:** Reserve burn rate exceeds 150% of plan → triggers reserve replenishment strategy.

**Risk Reserve Replenishment:**

If reserve depletes faster than plan, options include:

1. **Harvest Cost Opportunities:**
   - Accelerate supplier cost reduction initiatives
   - Pursue automation ROI faster than planned
   - Implement lean manufacturing early to free up cash

2. **Request Contract Modification:**
   - If risk realization is due to customer-directed changes or GFE delays
   - Negotiate equitable adjustment (REA) per FAR 52.243-4
   - Typical recovery: 80-90% of contractor cost impact

3. **Corporate Support:**
   - Request additional management reserve from corporate (last resort)
   - Typically requires executive committee approval
   - May impact program VP's performance incentives

#### **Metric 4: Earned Value Management (EVM) – CPI and SPI**

**What It Measures:** Cost and schedule performance against baseline plan

**Why It Matters:**
- Required by government contracts (FAR compliance)
- Early indicator of cost overruns or schedule delays
- Predicts Estimate at Completion (EAC)

**How It's Calculated:**
```
CPI (Cost Performance Index) = Earned Value (EV) / Actual Cost (AC)
SPI (Schedule Performance Index) = Earned Value (EV) / Planned Value (PV)

Where:
EV = Budgeted cost of work performed
AC = Actual cost incurred
PV = Budgeted cost of work scheduled
```

**Interpretation:**
- CPI >1.0 = Under budget (good)
- CPI <1.0 = Over budget (bad)
- SPI >1.0 = Ahead of schedule (good)
- SPI <1.0 = Behind schedule (bad)

**Reporting Frequency:** Monthly

**Target:**
- CPI ≥0.95 (not more than 5% over budget)
- SPI ≥0.95 (not more than 5% behind schedule)

**EAC Forecast:**
```
EAC = Budget at Completion (BAC) / CPI

Example: 
If BAC = $4,380M and CPI = 0.97
Then EAC = $4,380M / 0.97 = $4,515M (Overrun of $135M)
```

**Action Triggers:**
- **Green:** CPI ≥0.98, SPI ≥0.98 → Maintain course
- **Yellow:** CPI or SPI 0.95-0.98 → Implement corrective action plan
- **Red:** CPI or SPI <0.95 → Executive intervention, rebaseline if necessary

### 7.2 Risk Quantification Methodology

**Probabilistic Cost Risk Analysis:**

**Step 1: Identify Cost Risk Drivers**

For each WBS element, identify key cost risk drivers:
- Labor productivity uncertainty
- Material price volatility
- Supplier cost growth
- Rework rates

**Step 2: Assign Probability Distributions**

| Risk Driver | Distribution Type | Parameters (Example) |
|-------------|------------------|----------------------|
| Labor hours per unit | Triangular | Min: -10%, Most Likely: 0%, Max: +25% |
| Material cost escalation | Normal | Mean: +2.5%, Std Dev: 1.5% |
| Rework rate | Beta | α=2, β=8 (pessimistic skew) |

**Step 3: Monte Carlo Simulation**

- Run 10,000 iterations of program cost model
- Each iteration randomly samples from risk driver distributions
- Output: Probability distribution of total program cost (EAC)

**Step 4: Risk Reserve Sizing**

```
Risk Reserve = P70 EAC - P50 EAC

Where:
P50 = 50th percentile (median) cost estimate
P70 = 70th percentile cost estimate (30% chance of exceeding)
```

**Example Output:**

|------------|--------------------------|------------------|

| P25 | $4,265M | -$115M |
| P50 (Median) | $4,380M | $0 (baseline) |
| P70 | $4,505M | **$125M** (risk reserve target) |
| P90 | $4,685M | $305M |

**Interpretation:**
- Baseline EAC: $4,380M (P50)
- Risk Reserve Required: $125M (P70 - P50)

## 10. EQUITABLE ADJUSTMENTS AND CUSTOMER-CAUSED COST IMPACTS

### 10.1 Equitable Adjustment (REA) Strategy

**Definition:**

An **Equitable Adjustment (EA)** is a contract price or schedule adjustment due to customer-caused changes, delays, or impacts beyond contractor control. In FFP contracts, EAs are the primary mechanism to recover costs caused by:
- Government-furnished equipment (GFE) delivery delays
- Customer-directed requirement changes (Class I)
- Government-caused schedule disruptions (e.g., test range unavailability)
- Differing site conditions (for infrastructure contracts; less relevant to aircraft programs)

**EA Philosophy:**

FFP contracts place performance risk on the contractor, **but not customer-caused risk**. Eurus will aggressively pursue EAs for legitimate customer-caused impacts to protect program margin.

---

### 10.2 EA Identification and Quantification Process

**Step 1: Impact Event Identification**

| Event Type | Example | Contractual Basis for EA |
|------------|---------|--------------------------|
| **GFE Delay** | Government-provided mission computer delivered 6 months late | FAR 52.245-1 (Government Property) |
| **Requirement Change** | Customer directs KPP change post-CDR | FAR 52.243-1 (Changes - Fixed Price) |
| **Test Resource Unavailability** | Government test range unavailable for 3 months due to other program priority | Contract Schedule Annex, Government-furnished services |
| **Data Delivery Delay** | Customer late in providing threat data required for defensive systems design | Statement of Work (SOW), Government-furnished information (GFI) |

**Step 2: Cost Impact Quantification**

**EA Cost Buildup (Example: GFE Mission Computer Delay)**

| Impact Category | Cost Element | Calculation | Amount |
|-----------------|--------------|-------------|--------|
| **Direct Labor** | Extended engineering team (6 months delay) | 12 FTEs × 6 months × $18K/month | $1,296K |
| **Overhead** | Burden on extended labor | $1,296K × 125% | $1,620K |
| **Facilities** | Lab space, tooling idle time | 6 months × $45K/month | $270K |
| **Supplier Impacts** | Supplier pause/restart costs, expedite fees | Documented supplier claims | $420K |
| **Schedule Compression** | Overtime to recover schedule post-GFE delivery | 8,000 hours × $95/hr (OT premium) | $760K |
| **Material Obsolescence** | Parts procured early now obsolete due to delay | Scrap cost + re-procurement | $185K |
| **Subtotal Direct Costs** | | | $4,551K |
| **G&A Allocation** | Corporate overhead allocation | $4,551K × 8% | $364K |
| **Subtotal Cost** | | | $4,915K |
| **Profit/Fee** | Reasonable profit on EA work | $4,915K × 10% | $492K |
| **Total EA Claim** | | | **$5,407K** |

**Step 3: Schedule Impact Quantification**

- **Critical Path Analysis:** Demonstrate GFE delay is on the critical path → program schedule slip of 4 months
- **Liquidated Damage Exposure:** 4-month delivery delay triggers $8M in LDs (2M/month × 4 months)
- **EA Schedule Relief Request:** Request 4-month contract schedule extension to avoid LD penalties

---

### 10.3 EA Negotiation and Settlement

**EA Claim Package Contents:**

1. **Narrative Description:** Detailed explanation of customer-caused event and impact
2. **Contractual Basis:** Citation of contract clauses supporting EA entitlement
3. **Cost Impact Analysis:** Detailed cost buildup with supporting documentation (timecards, invoices, supplier claims)
4. **Schedule Impact Analysis:** Critical path analysis, schedule logic diagrams, resource-loaded schedule
5. **Mitigation Efforts:** Demonstration that Eurus took reasonable steps to minimize impact
6. **Proposed Resolution:** Requested contract price adjustment and/or schedule extension

**EA Negotiation Strategy:**

| Element | Eurus Position | Customer Likely Position | Negotiation Strategy |
|---------|----------------|--------------------------|----------------------|
| **Causation** | GFE delay is 100% customer-caused | Contractor should have anticipated delay, mitigated impact | **Provide Evidence:** Contract delivery dates, customer correspondence acknowledging delay |
| **Cost Quantum** | $5.4M fully burdened cost + profit | $3.2M (direct costs only, no profit) | **Compromise:** Settle at $4.5M (includes overhead and reduced profit margin ~8%) |
| **Schedule Relief** | 4-month extension (full critical path impact) | 2-month extension (partial concurrency recovery) | **Negotiate:** 3-month extension + accept $2M LD waiver for remaining 1 month |
| **Profit/Fee** | 10% profit on EA costs (standard) | 0% profit (equitable adjustment should be cost-neutral) | **Compromise:** 6-8% profit (compensates for disruption, financing costs) |

**Settlement Outcomes:**

| Outcome | Contract Price Adjustment | Schedule Adjustment | Net Financial Impact (to Eurus) |
|---------|---------------------------|---------------------|----------------------------------|
| **Full Claim Accepted** | +$5.4M | +4 months | +$5.4M margin improvement (LD avoided: $8M; net gain: $5.4M) |
| **Negotiated Settlement** | +$4.5M | +3 months | +$2.5M margin improvement (LD exposure: $2M; net: $4.5M - $2M LD = $2.5M) |
| **Claim Rejected** | $0 | 0 months | -$8M margin erosion ($4.9M absorbed cost + $8M LD paid = -$12.9M; partially offset by baseline contingency) |

**Eurus Approach:** Aggressively document and pursue EAs, but settle pragmatically to avoid litigation costs and customer relationship damage.

### 11.5 Lessons Learned and Best Practices (Financial Perspective)

**What Worked Well:**

1. **Conservative Learning Curve Assumption (85%):**
   - Actual learning curve: 82%
   - Result: $26M direct labor cost beat
   - **Lesson:** Conservative assumptions in FFP programs provide margin cushion; actual performance often beats pessimistic plans

2. **Aggressive Supplier Cost Management:**
   - Multi-year agreements locked in favorable pricing
   - Should-cost analysis drove competitive negotiations
   - Result: $22M supplier cost beat
   - **Lesson:** Invest in supplier relationships and cost analytics early (PM 18-24); ROI is 10:1

3. **Early Risk Mitigation Investment:**
   - $15M invested in automation (PM 24-36)
   - Delivered $95M learning curve improvement (6.3× ROI)
   - **Lesson:** Risk mitigation that reduces recurring costs pays for itself many times over in production phase

4. **Disciplined Change Control:**
   - Only 3.2% of contract value in changes (vs. 5% industry average)
   - 70% of changes were customer-funded (vs. 60% industry average)
   - Result: Avoided $40M in unfunded requirement creep
   - **Lesson:** Rigorous CCB process and customer engagement on cost impacts prevents scope creep

5. **Proactive EA Pursuit:**
   - Identified and documented $18.3M in customer-caused impacts
   - Recovered 66% ($11M) through negotiated settlements
   - **Lesson:** Document impacts in real-time; don't wait until program end to pursue EAs

**What Could Have Been Better:**

1. **Contract Mod Pipeline Execution:**
   - Target: $150M in mods by IOC
   - Actual: $123M executed (82% of target)
   - **Gap Analysis:** P00002 (Weapon System Integration, $42M) delayed due to customer budget constraints
   - **Lesson:** Mod pipeline forecasts should include customer budget probability assessment; don't assume all mods will execute

2. **Risk Reserve Sizing:**
   - Allocated: $74M (1.5% of contract)
   - Recommended (P70): $125M (2.6% of contract)
   - Actual Burn: $62M (1.3% of contract)
   - **Lesson:** Program got "lucky" — actual risk realization was below P50. Reserve was undersized relative to statistical recommendation, but execution quality mitigated risk. Future programs should target P70 reserve level.

3. **DSO Management:**
   - Target: 50 days
   - Actual Peak: 58 days (during LRIP transition)
   - Impact: $18M negative cash flow spike (required corporate line of credit draw)
   - **Lesson:** Customer payment lag during production ramp is predictable; negotiate milestone payment structure to front-load cash during LRIP

4. **Warranty Cost Underestimation:**
   - Baseline: $8M warranty reserve
   - Actual: $12M warranty costs (first 12 months post-delivery)
   - **Gap:** Rotor blade vibration issue required retrofit on 8 aircraft
   - **Lesson:** Warranty reserves for new aircraft programs should be 0.3-0.5% of contract value (vs. 0.15% initially allocated)

## 13. TAX STRATEGY AND ACCOUNTING TREATMENT

### 13.1 Revenue Recognition Method

**Accounting Standard:** ASC 606 (Revenue from Contracts with Customers)

**Revenue Recognition Method for MAAP-1 FFP Contract:**

**Method:** **Percentage-of-Completion (POC)** using **Cost-to-Cost Input Method**

**Rationale:**
- FFP contract with defined milestones and deliverables → revenue recognized as performance obligations are satisfied over time
- Cost-to-Cost method: Revenue = (Cumulative Costs Incurred / Total Estimated Costs at Completion) × Total Contract Value
- Alternative methods considered and rejected:
  - **Units-of-Delivery:** Not appropriate; significant NRE in early phases, would distort revenue recognition
  - **Milestone Method:** Not appropriate; milestones are unevenly spaced and not proportional to value delivered

**Revenue Recognition Example (Simplified):**

| Program Month | Cumulative Costs Incurred | Total EAC | POC % | Total Contract Value | Cumulative Revenue Recognized | Current Period Revenue |
|---------------|---------------------------|-----------|-------|----------------------|------------------------------|------------------------|
| PM 12 | $150M | $4,380M | 3.4% | $4,778M | $163M | $163M |
| PM 24 | $420M | $4,380M | 9.6% | $4,778M | $459M | $296M |
| PM 60 | $1,850M | $4,380M | 42.2% | $4,778M | $2,017M | $1,558M (cumulative since PM 24) |

**Contract Liability (Deferred Revenue):**

- If milestone payments received exceed POC revenue recognized → Contract Liability on balance sheet
- If POC revenue recognized exceeds milestone payments received → Contract Asset (unbilled receivables) on balance sheet

**Example at PM 24:**

- Cumulative milestone payments received: $500M (per Section 2.2 payment schedule)
- POC revenue recognized: $459M
- **Contract Liability: $41M** (customer has paid in advance of work performed)

**EAC Changes and Revenue Impact:**

- If EAC increases (cost overrun) → POC percentage decreases for same costs incurred → Revenue recognition slows → **Margin erosion immediately recognized**
- If EAC decreases (cost beat) → POC percentage increases → Revenue recognition accelerates → **Margin improvement immediately recognized**

**Example: EAC Increase at PM 36:**

- Previous EAC: $4,380M
- Revised EAC: $4,485M (due to supplier cost overrun)
- Cumulative costs incurred: $850M
- Previous POC%: 850 / 4,380 = 19.4% → Revenue: $928M
- Revised POC%: 850 / 4,485 = 18.9% → Revenue: $903M
- **Revenue reversal: $25M** (reduces current period revenue)
- **Margin impact: -$105M** (EAC growth of $105M flows immediately to bottom line)

**Financial Statement Impact:**

This is why **EAC management is critical in FFP programs** — every dollar of cost growth immediately hits the income statement as reduced margin, even if the cost hasn't been incurred yet (due to POC revenue recognition adjustment).

- Facility improvements: $120M (15-year MACRS property)

**Standard Depreciation:** Straight-line over asset life (e.g., 5 years for tooling)

**Accelerated Depreciation (MACRS):** 200% declining balance method → higher deductions in early years

**Bonus Depreciation (if applicable):** 100% first-year expensing for qualified property (per Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, phasing down after 2022)

**Tax Deferral Benefit:**
- Accelerated depreciation defers taxes to later years (time value of money benefit)
- Example: $215M tooling investment
  - Straight-line: $43M/year depreciation over 5 years
  - MACRS 200% DB: $86M (Year 1), $52M (Year 2), $31M (Year 3), etc.

### 13.3 Deferred Tax Assets and Liabilities

**Deferred Tax Assets (DTAs):**

DTAs arise when tax deductions occur **before** book (GAAP) expenses. Common in aerospace programs:

| Source | Book Treatment | Tax Treatment | DTA Amount (Example at PM 60) |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| **Warranty Reserve** | Expense recognized at delivery (accrual basis) | Deductible when paid (cash basis) | $12M warranty reserve × 28% = $3.4M DTA |
| **Deferred Revenue (Contract Liability)** | Revenue deferred until earned (POC) | Taxable when cash received (milestone payments) | $41M contract liability × 28% = $11.5M DTA |
| **Stock-Based Compensation** | Expense recognized when granted (GAAP) | Deductible when exercised (tax) | $5M unvested stock comp × 28% = $1.4M DTA |

**Total DTAs (Program-Related):** ~$16M at PM 60 (midpoint of program)

**Deferred Tax Liabilities (DTLs):**

DTLs arise when tax deductions occur **after** book expenses:

| Source | Book Treatment | Tax Treatment | DTL Amount (Example at PM 60) |
|--------|----------------|---------------|-------------------------------|
| **Accelerated Depreciation (Tooling)** | Straight-line depreciation (GAAP) | MACRS accelerated (tax) | $35M excess tax depreciation × 28% = $9.8M DTL |
| **R&D Capitalization (post-2022 tax law)** | Expensed as incurred (GAAP) | Capitalized and amortized over 5 years (tax) | $85M capitalized R&D × 28% = $23.8M DTL |

**Total DTLs (Program-Related):** ~$34M at PM 60

**Net Deferred Tax Liability:** $34M DTL - $16M DTA = **$18M net DTL**

**Financial Statement Impact:**
- Net DTL is a **balance sheet liability** (taxes owed in future periods when temporary differences reverse)
- Does not affect cash flow in current period (non-cash item)
- Reverses over time as depreciation normalizes and revenue/expense timing aligns

### 13.4 International Tax Considerations (for Export Sales)

**Foreign Sales Structure:**

For international sales (Section 11.6, O-FO-3), Eurus Systems will structure transactions to optimize tax efficiency:

**Option 1: Direct FMS Sales (U.S. Government as Intermediary):**
- U.S. Government purchases aircraft from Eurus, resells to foreign government via Foreign Military Sales (FMS) program
- **Tax Treatment:** Domestic U.S. sale → fully taxable in U.S. at 28% effective rate
- **Benefit:** No foreign tax withholding, simplified export compliance
- **Downside:** No tax optimization; full U.S. tax burden

**Option 2: Direct Commercial Sale (via Foreign Subsidiary):**
- Eurus establishes foreign subsidiary (e.g., Eurus International Ltd. in Ireland or Luxembourg)
- Subsidiary acts as distributor: purchases aircraft from U.S. parent, resells to foreign customer
- **Tax Treatment:** 
  - Transfer pricing: U.S. parent sells to subsidiary at cost + markup (taxed in U.S.)
  - Subsidiary profit on resale taxed in foreign jurisdiction (e.g., Ireland 12.5% corporate tax rate)
- **Benefit:** Lower effective tax rate on foreign sales profit (12.5% vs. 28%)
- **Downside:** Transfer pricing complexity, potential IRS scrutiny, ITAR export license requirements

**Recommendation:**
- Use **FMS structure** for initial export sales (simplicity, customer preference, reduced regulatory risk)
- Consider **foreign subsidiary structure** if export volume exceeds $1B cumulative (justifies transfer pricing infrastructure investment)

**Withholding Tax on Royalties/Technical Assistance:**

If Eurus licenses technology to foreign partners or provides technical assistance:
- Foreign governments may withhold tax on royalty/service payments (typically 10-30%)
- U.S. tax treaties may reduce withholding rates (e.g., U.S.-UK treaty: 0% on royalties)
- **Mitigation:** Structure agreements to qualify for treaty benefits; claim foreign tax credit on U.S. return

### 14.3 Warranty Reserve and Product Liability Exposure

**Warranty Terms (Standard MAAP-1 Contract):**

- **Airframe and Systems:** 12 months or 500 flight hours, whichever first
- **Engines:** 3,500 flight hours or 5 years (manufacturer's warranty passed through)
- **Avionics LRUs:** 24 months or 1,000 flight hours (supplier warranty passed through)

**Warranty Reserve Calculation:**

Warranty costs are **accrued at delivery** (GAAP requirement) based on estimated future warranty claims.

**Estimation Methodology:**

```
Warranty Reserve per Aircraft = (Estimated Warranty Cost Rate) × (Delivered Aircraft Count)

Where:
Estimated Warranty Cost Rate = Historical Claims Rate × Aircraft Value × Warranty Period
```

**MAAP-1 Warranty Reserve:**

- **Historical Benchmark:** Comparable rotorcraft programs: 0.8-1.5% of aircraft flyaway cost
- **MAAP-1 Assumption:** 1.2% of flyaway cost (conservative, given autonomous system complexity and limited fleet data)
- **Flyaway Cost per Aircraft (FRP Lot 5):** $21.5M
- **Warranty Reserve per Aircraft:** $21.5M × 1.2% = **$258K per aircraft**
- **Total Program Warranty Reserve (183 production aircraft):** $47.2M

**Warranty Reserve Accounting:**

| Event | Debit | Credit | Balance Sheet Impact |
|-------|-------|--------|----------------------|
| **Aircraft Delivery** | Warranty Expense ($258K) | Warranty Reserve Liability ($258K) | Liability increases |
| **Warranty Claim Paid** | Warranty Reserve Liability ($50K) | Cash ($50K) | Liability decreases |
| **Reserve Adjustment (if actual < estimated)** | Warranty Reserve Liability ($100K) | Warranty Expense Recovery ($100K) | Income increase (reversal of excess reserve) |

**Product Liability Exposure (Long-Tail Risk):**

Unlike warranty (12-month coverage), product liability claims can arise **decades after delivery** (e.g., crashworthiness litigation 15 years post-delivery).

**Mitigation Strategies:**

1. **Insurance:** $500M product liability policy (Section 14.1) covers catastrophic claims
2. **Contract Indemnification:** Government customer agrees to indemnify Eurus for claims arising from government-directed changes or operational use (standard FAR clause)
3. **Statutory Liability Limits:** General Aviation Revitalization Act (GARA) limits liability for aircraft >18 years old (not applicable to MAAP-1 as helicopter, but similar state statutes may apply)
4. **Product Liability Reserve:** Eurus accrues a **long-tail product liability reserve** ($3M per delivered aircraft over 30-year horizon), discounted to present value

**Total Product Liability Reserve (MAAP-1):** $85M (present value, 5% discount rate)
